% ===================== 1. INTRODUCTION =====================
\section{Introduction}
Premature ventricular complexes (PVCs) are early ventricular depolarizations that
occur before the next expected sinus beat. They originate below the atrioventricular
node, from the ventricular myocardium or His--Purkinje system, and typically appear
on the ECG as premature, morphologically distinct QRS complexes. PVCs are common and may occur in individuals with or without structural heart disease. In many cases they
are benign, especially when infrequent and occurring in a structurally normal heart;
however, their clinical relevance depends on symptoms, burden, morphology, coupling
pattern, presence of repetitive forms, and the underlying cardiac substrate.

Frequent PVCs can cause palpitations, skipped-beat sensations, or a forceful post-extrasystolic beat, but they may also be asymptomatic. From a clinical perspective, the main
objectives are to quantify PVC burden, assess whether the ectopy is monomorphic or
polymorphic, identify couplets or non-sustained ventricular tachycardia, and exclude
structural or functional heart disease. High PVC burden has been associated with
PVC-induced or PVC-aggravated cardiomyopathy, particularly when ectopy is persistent
over time. For this reason, formal evaluation requires validated ECG monitoring,
cardiac imaging when indicated, and interpretation by qualified clinicians.

This report makes no such clinical evaluation. 

% ===================== 2. HARDWARE =====================
\section{Hardware}
The recorder is built around an AD8232 single-lead biopotential front-end, with three
snap electrodes arranged in a single precordial derivation. Its output is digitised by
the internal 12-bit ADC of a Raspberry Pi Pico 2 W (channel \texttt{ADC0\,/\,GP26})
running MicroPython, and sampled at 250\,Hz. The samples are streamed wirelessly over
Wi-Fi, or over USB serial when the device is tethered. The recorder can operate as an untethered, battery-powered wireless unit, using a single-cell LiPo battery with a TP4056 charging module and an SPDT power switch, providing approximately 24\,h of continuous recording per charge.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Current implementation and near-term upgrades.} The recorder is an evolving prototype, and the
present streaming-only architecture is a deliberate first stage. At present, the Pico does not store recordings locally. It streams samples in real time to a receiving host, which writes them to disk. The host can be a laptop connected over USB serial or a server on the same Wi-Fi network. As a result, a host must currently be available and within range during acquisition. 

The components were nevertheless selected with a self-contained, body-worn recorder as the goal. The Raspberry Pi Pico 2 W was chosen for its integrated Wi-Fi and low power consumption, which already allow operation as a battery-powered wireless acquisition node and provide a clear path toward onboard logging. The AD8232 front-end and the single-cell LiPo battery keep the unit compact, rechargeable, and suitable for portable untethered acquisition (device size: $\sim$ 8 $\times$ 6 $\times$ 3\,cm; the assembled unit is shown in \textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:hw}}). At this stage, streaming to a laptop is the simplest way to validate the full acquisition, storage, visualisation, and analysis chain.

This is being implemented by adding a 3\,V microSD breakout module, the Adafruit 4682, to the Pico over SPI and having the firmware log each session locally. AA standard 32\,GB microSD card would provide storage for months of ECG recordings, allowing sessions to be recorded independently of any external computer during acquisition. The Wi-Fi stream could still remain available for live monitoring through the dashboard, including from a smartphone connected to the same network.

A dual-accelerometer extension, based on two ADXL345 modules, is also planned. One sensor would be positioned on the sternum and one on the abdomen, allowing posture to be estimated from the gravity vector and respiration to be measured directly from thoraco-abdominal movement. This would complement the present report, where respiration is estimated indirectly from R-wave amplitude (see Section~\ref{sec:edr}) and posture is tracked manually during recording.

% ===================== 3. SOFTWARE =====================
\section{Software}
On the host side --- currently a MacBook --- a Python application receives the stream from the recorder, stores the ECG signal, and serves the live trace through a Flask dashboard using Server-Sent Events. The dashboard can be opened either on the MacBook itself or on a smartphone connected to the same network, allowing the trace to be followed in real time during acquisition.

The same dashboard is used to add time-stamped manual annotations during recording, marking events such as meals, caffeine intake, physical exertion, posture changes, or perceived symptoms. These annotations are stored within the session and remain aligned with the ECG trace during later analysis.

A set of batch tools then regenerates the data-derived parts of the analysis --- every figure, table, and summary statistic --- directly from the stored recordings, and the figures and tables in this document are produced by that pipeline. Adding a new recording and re-running updates all of them coherently. The written analysis, interpretation, and conclusions, by contrast, are authored by hand and are not generated automatically: the tooling keeps the figures and numbers in sync with the data, while their interpretation remains the author's. The same software environment includes the signal-processing, beat-detection, PVC-classification, manual-curation, and export routines described in the following sections.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Code, analysis, and hardware documentation.} The recorder firmware, the full signal-processing and analysis pipeline used to generate every figure and table in this report, and the complete hardware build documentation, including wiring diagrams and bill of materials, are available at \href{https://github.com/mrEg0n/holter-ecg}{\texttt{github.com/mrEg0n/holter-ecg}}.

% ===================== RECORDING QUALITY (prima) =====================
\section{Recording quality \& PVC auto-detection}\label{sec:quality}
Beat detection and classification are fully automatic. The ECG signal is first band-pass filtered online between 0.3 and 25\,Hz and sampled at 250\,Hz. QRS complexes are then detected by a custom finite-state-machine detector with a 300\,ms refractory period. Each detected beat is subsequently labelled as sinus or ectopic by a fixed rule-based classifier.

A beat is flagged as a premature ventricular complex (PVC) when it satisfies predefined criteria based on QRS width, post-QRS rebound, signal amplitude, and physiologically plausible duration. Specifically, PVC candidates must show sufficient width and/or a marked post-QRS rebound, adequate amplitude, and a measured width within the 40--220\,ms range. No template matching or model training is used. The same thresholds are applied unchanged across all sessions, ensuring that every recording is processed with the same classification rule.

These thresholds were tuned and empirically validated on this single subject. They therefore reflect this individual's ECG morphology, signal amplitude, electrode placement, and acquisition geometry. The same parametrization should not be assumed to transfer unchanged to other subjects. In practice, the most subject-dependent parameter is the absolute amplitude threshold. This could be made self-calibrating by estimating it once from a clean baseline window at the beginning of a recording and then holding it fixed throughout the session. Such a baseline-then-fixed approach would avoid continuous adaptation, which could otherwise be transiently biased by motion artefacts or poor electrode contact. With this modification, the same pipeline could in principle be extended to other subjects recorded with comparable electrode placement. A complementary future direction is to add a dedicated QRS-enhancement stage to the detector: a Pan--Tompkins-style 5--15\,Hz band-pass followed by derivative, squaring, and moving-window integration with adaptive thresholds, run on a detection-only signal path while the present wider band is kept for beat morphology. This energy-based detection would further reduce the dependence on the absolute amplitude threshold and recover the occasional low-amplitude sinus beat that the current threshold-based detector can miss.

The only operation left to manual curation is the exclusion of intervals corrupted by motion artefacts or poor electrode contact. These intervals are marked by hand in a paginated editor, which stores per-session exclusion files. Beats falling within excluded intervals are removed before any downstream analysis.

\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:quality}} illustrates the detection and curation procedure on a continuous segment from one recording, displayed as consecutive 12\,s rows. The trace is stable over most of the segment, and each detected beat carries its automatic label, with PVCs shown in red and sinus beats in green. A single shaded interval corresponds to a burst of motion/contact noise and is visually distinct from the surrounding ECG. This is the type of segment removed during manual noise curation.

\section{PVC characterization and morphology}
Across the recordings, PVCs occur in several recurring patterns, including isolated
beats, couplets, short non-sustained runs, and bigeminal stretches.
\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:examples}} shows representative $\approx$14\,s windows selected
automatically across sessions and kept away from manually excluded noise intervals. The
same automatic labelling used throughout the report is overlaid on the trace: PVC QRS
complexes are highlighted in red over a $\pm$120\,ms window and marked
individually, while sinus beats are marked in green.

\reportfig{example_strips.png}{Representative clean windows ($\approx$14\,s) selected automatically across sessions, covering the main PVC presentations: isolated beats, couplets, and short non-sustained runs (including a bigeminal stretch). Detector output overlaid --- PVC QRS in red with a marker, sinus beats tagged green. The event type and session time are printed above each panel.\label{fig:examples}}

A total of \totPVC{} PVCs were detected across the \nSessions{} recordings. Within the limits of a single-lead recording setup, the analysis asked whether the detected PVCs shared a common electrical signature, consistent with a single dominant monomorphic ectopic source rather than multiple clearly distinct PVC morphologies. To this end the complexes were characterised morphologically: each PVC was
centred on its ectopic peak, normalised to its own amplitude (peak $=1.0$) and
superimposed, with the median and inter-quartile band over the whole set and one median
waveform per session (\textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:morph}a,b}). The session medians were then compared
quantitatively by pairwise correlation (\textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:morph}c}), and the firing timing
summarised by the coupling-interval distribution (\textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:morph}d}).

The superimposed complexes collapse onto a single tight shape and the per-session medians
are almost indistinguishable: every pair of medians correlates at $r\geq0.992$ (median
$r=0.997$). One recording (2026-06-06 13:41) shows a marginally deeper hyperpolarisation
trough (\textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:morph}b}) but still correlates at $r\geq0.99$, so the difference is
amplitude / repolarisation variation rather than a distinct complex. The coupling interval
is correspondingly tight (median 480\,ms, IQR 460--504\,ms). Together, these data are
compatible with a single dominant monomorphic, rather than multiple clearly distinguishable PVC morphologies.

\reportfig{02_pvc_morphology_summary.png}{Morphology of all detected PVCs. (a) every PVC overlaid (amplitude-normalised) with the median and IQR; (b) one median waveform per session; (c) pairwise correlation matrix of the session medians (colour: Pearson $r$, scale 0.95--1.0; all $r\geq0.99$); (d) coupling-interval distribution (median 480\,ms).\label{fig:morph}}

\subsection{Assessment of continuous versus discrete PVC morphology}
The overlay and pairwise correlation analyses provide no evidence for a second PVC morphology
with a clearly distinct QRS morphology. However, these measures are less sensitive to a subtler
possibility: two highly similar morphological subtypes whose session medians remain strongly
correlated and therefore appear as a single continuous population. High mean correlation is
therefore necessary but not sufficient to support a single-source interpretation, since it
could also arise from two tightly overlapping subpopulations.

To test for discrete structure directly, rather than infer it only from morphological
similarity, the full set of \totPVC{} amplitude-normalised PVC complexes was examined using
four complementary checks, each probing clustering from a different angle
(\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:continuum}}):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{PCA + K-means (k = 2).} A true two-subtype structure would be expected to
        produce two separated clouds, whereas a connected cloud split through its centre
        would suggest an artificial partition imposed by the algorithm
        (\textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:continuum}a}).
  \item \textbf{Sorting by hyperpolarisation depth.} Two sharp bands would support discrete
        subtypes, whereas a smooth progression would support continuous variability
        (\textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:continuum}b}).
  \item \textbf{K-means elbow plot.} A sharp knee would suggest a natural number of clusters,
        whereas a smooth decay would argue against discrete clustering
        (\textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:continuum}c}).
  \item \textbf{Trough-depth histogram.} A unimodal distribution would be consistent with a
        single continuous population, whereas two separated peaks would support discrete
        morphological subtypes (\textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:continuum}d}).
\end{itemize}

All four analyses point in the same direction: the PVCs form a single continuous population
rather than separable morphological classes. The PCA projection shows one connected cloud, and
forcing $k=2$ only divides it along the main axis of variation, producing two partitions of
6,667 and 10,621 beats without a visible gap between them. The depth-sorted heatmap changes
gradually from shallower to deeper troughs, with no clear band boundary. The elbow curve decays
smoothly, without a distinct knee, and the trough-depth distribution is unimodal. Within the
limits of a single-lead recording setup, this pattern is consistent with a single dominant
monomorphic ectopic source showing continuous beat-to-beat variability, rather than multiple
clearly distinguishable PVC morphologies.

\reportfig{03_pvc_continuum_check.png}{Single-population checks. (a) PCA + K-means projection; (b) PVCs sorted by hyperpolarisation depth; (c) the K-means elbow; (d) the trough-depth histogram.\label{fig:continuum}}

\textbf{Note on the sparse points (lower-right of the PCA panel, Fig.~\ref{fig:continuum}a).} The thin
scatter fanning out below the dense ball does not show the features expected of a separate
morphological class or a clearly distinct ectopic source:
it is a low-density skirt of more variable beats (about 3\% of the total),
concentrated in the two shortest / noisiest recordings, with a median QRS
essentially identical to the core's. It reflects beat-to-beat / baseline
variability captured by PC2 --- consistent with the single-population picture
from the elbow and the unimodal trough histogram. Additional clustering-robustness
checks --- silhouette across $k$, Gaussian-mixture and density-based clustering, and a
direct profile of these sparse beats --- are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:clust} and
reinforce this interpretation.

% ===================== COUPLING / SINGLE-SOURCE CHECK =====================
\section{Per-session coupling distribution and single-source consistency check}
The coupling interval, \rrpre{}, is the time between the preceding sinus beat and the PVC. It
measures how long after a sinus beat the ectopic complex occurs. A stable monomorphic PVC source
is expected to show a relatively tight coupling distribution. By contrast, multiple sources or
clearly distinct ectopic mechanisms may produce separate coupling peaks, particularly if
associated with different QRS morphologies.

For each session the coupling distribution was examined for evidence of more than one coupling
mode (\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:coupling}}). Across sessions the distribution ranged on a continuum,
from a single mode with a right-skewed shoulder to a more clearly separated second mode, without a
sharp boundary between the two cases. Rather than forcing a binary bimodal-versus-unimodal label,
each distribution was split into a shorter- and a longer-coupling cluster --- at the trough where
two modes were clearly separated, otherwise at the midpoint between the two fitted Gaussian-mixture
modes --- and the QRS morphology of the two clusters was then compared.

In every session the two coupling clusters had essentially identical QRS morphology (template
correlation $r\geq0.99$ throughout). Within the limits of a single-lead recording setup, this is
consistent with coupling-interval modulation of a single dominant monomorphic focus --- the same
complex recurring at two coupling intervals --- rather than a morphologically distinct second
source. No session showed a coupling mode associated with a clearly distinct QRS morphology, so the
per-session coupling analysis remains consistent with the single-population interpretation derived
from morphology, clustering, and cross-session correlation.

\reportfig{12_per_session_coupling_distribution.png}{Per-session coupling-interval distributions with the statistical single-source consistency check based on Gaussian mixture modelling.\label{fig:coupling}}

\section{Normal-beat morphology}
As a non-ectopic baseline, the same amplitude-normalised superimposition was applied to the
normal sinus beats (N), sampling up to 500 evenly spaced beats per session. Beyond serving as a
reference morphology, the N population is a sensitive probe for electrode-placement and posture
effects: its R-wave amplitude varies visibly with respiration, which is exploited later
for respiration (Section~\ref{sec:edr}). The beats were overlaid with their median and IQR
(\textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:nmorph}a}), reduced to one median per session
(\textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:nmorph}b}), and compared across sessions by pairwise correlation
(\textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:nmorph}c}).

The cross-session N medians are highly concordant, with all pairwise correlations $r\geq0.93$.
One session, 2026-06-09 13:23, is consistently the least similar to the others, showing the
lowest mean correlation against the remaining sessions ($r=0.962$; \textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:nmorph}d}).
This was also the only recording acquired while seated at a desk with anterior trunk flexion;
the other sessions were acquired in lying or more extended postures.

This posture difference may have changed the projection of the cardiac electrical axis onto the
single precordial electrode, possibly explaining the observed drift in N morphology. The effect
appears more visible in normal beats than in PVCs because the PVC waveform has a large dominant
deflection, approximately 1.5\,V, whereas the smaller and more symmetric N waveform,
approximately 0.5\,V, gives proportionally greater weight to the P- and T-wave regions. This
remains an interpretation of the data, not a confirmed mechanism.

% ===================== 5. INTERP vs COMP =====================
\section{Interpolated versus compensated PVCs}
Each PVC is evaluated in relation to the preceding and following sinus beats, forming an
N--PVC--N sequence. The distinction between interpolated and compensated PVCs is based on how
the ectopic beat fits into the surrounding sinus rhythm, measured from QRS peak to QRS peak. An
interpolated PVC occurs between two sinus beats without producing a substantial delay
of the next sinus beat, so the N--PVC--N interval remains close to one local sinus cycle. A
compensated PVC is followed by a longer post-extrasystolic pause, so the N--PVC--N
interval approaches approximately two local sinus cycles. Representative examples of both types are
shown in \textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:icex}}.

In this subject the two classes differ markedly in perceived impact. Interpolated PVCs are
usually not noticed, whereas compensated PVCs are followed by a pause and a stronger
post-extrasystolic sinus beat --- personally the most perceptible and bothersome component of
the ectopy.

\reportfig{06_interpolated_vs_compensated_example.png}{Two representative PVCs. (a) an interpolated beat, with \rrpost{} shorter than the local sinus cycle; (b) a compensated beat, with a longer post-extrasystolic pause. The gold bar marks the actual post-extrasystolic pause \rrpost{}; dashed lines mark $1\times$ (blue) and $2\times$ (red) the locally-estimated sinus cycle, and N\,prev\,/\,N\,next the flanking sinus beats.\label{fig:icex}}

The reference sinus cycle is not defined as a single session-wide value. Because this subject
shows marked respiratory sinus arrhythmia, the sinus cycle is estimated locally for
each PVC as the median of nearby N--N intervals. A prematurity guard then excludes PVC
candidates whose coupling interval, \rrpre{}, is not shorter than the local sinus cycle. Such
events are inconsistent with a truly premature ventricular beat and most likely reflect a
missed sub-threshold sinus beat within the interval. A total of 118 beats, approximately 1\% of
classified PVC candidates, were set aside by this guard.

Each PVC can be placed by the post-extrasystolic pause that follows it: most fall clearly into a
short-pause (interpolated) or long-pause (compensated) group, while a minority sit close to the
boundary. On a continuous strip (\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:strip3}}) these appear as interpolated
(blue), compensated (red), and the borderline intermediate beats (yellow).

\reportfig{interp_comp_3class_strip.png}{A continuous ECG strip classified by the post-extrasystolic pause \rrpost{}: interpolated (short pause, blue), compensated (long pause, red), and the borderline beats near the valley (yellow). Most PVCs are unambiguous; only a few sit at the boundary.\label{fig:strip3}}

A strict conventional sum rule (a ``full'' pause defined as N--PVC--N between $1.85$ and
$2.15\times$ the local sinus cycle) would leave a much broader grey zone. The dataset, however,
shows a clearer structure than that rule suggests.
\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:sdist}} compares two distributions: the conventional N--PVC--N sum, S,
and the actual post-extrasystolic pause, \rrpost{}, both expressed relative to the locally
estimated sinus cycle. Both distributions are bimodal, with most beats falling into one of two
modes and only a sparse valley between them.

\reportfig{07_distributions_of_s_and_rr_post.png}{Distributions of the conventional N--PVC--N sum, S (left), and the actual post-extrasystolic pause, \rrpost{} (right), both expressed relative to the locally estimated sinus cycle. Both measures show a bimodal structure.\label{fig:sdist}}

The conventional sum S and \rrpost{} agree for approximately 99\% of beats, but they diverge in
a small minority of cases, because the same N--PVC--N sum can correspond to different
post-extrasystolic pauses depending on the coupling interval. When the two measures disagree,
\rrpost{} better captures the operational distinction of interest: a short \rrpost{} corresponds
to an interpolated PVC, whereas a long \rrpost{} corresponds to a compensated PVC
followed by a clear pause.

For this reason, the final split was based on \rrpost{} rather than on the conventional sum
alone. The empirical valley between the two modes occurred at $\pauseValley\times$ the local
sinus cycle. Beats with \rrpost{} below this value were classified as interpolated, and
beats above it as compensated. Across the dataset this criterion classified
\pctInterp\% of measurable PVCs as interpolated and \pctComp\% as compensated,
based on \methodBeats{} classified beats. Only approximately 1\% of beats fell within $\pm0.10$
of the decision boundary; beats that remained genuinely ambiguous were not forced into either
class, being removed by the noise-exclusion procedure or by the prematurity guard.

The same classification is illustrated on a continuous ECG strip from session 2026-06-08 22:01
(\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:strip2}}).
Interpolated PVCs occur without a substantial gap in the surrounding sinus rhythm, whereas
compensated PVCs are followed by a visibly longer pause and a stronger post-extrasystolic beat.

% ===================== COUPLETS =====================
\section{Couplets and local rhythm structure}
A couplet was defined as two consecutive PVCs separated by an inter-PVC interval between 200 and
700\,ms and not belonging to a longer run. This represents the simplest form of repetitive
ventricular ectopy considered in this report. Couplets were counted separately because their
frequency, timing, and morphological uniformity provide additional information about the behaviour
of the detected PVC population.

Across the dataset, 46 couplets were detected in 7 of the 10 recording sessions; no couplets were
detected in the remaining 3 sessions. The inter-PVC interval was tightly distributed, with a
median of 416\,ms and a range of 384--464\,ms. The two beats within each couplet showed highly
similar morphology, consistent with repetitive activation from the same dominant monomorphic
ectopic source within the limits of a single-lead recording setup.

\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:couplets}} summarises the couplet analysis. The top panel (a) overlays all
couplets, aligned to the peak of the first PVC and amplitude-normalised, to assess their
morphological consistency. The lower-left panel (b) reports the number of couplets detected in each
session, and the lower-right panel (c) compares the median QRS waveform of the first PVC with that
of the second PVC within each couplet. The close overlap between the two median waveforms supports
the interpretation that the two beats in each couplet belong to the same recurrent PVC morphology.

\reportfig{13_couplets_count_and_overlay.png}{(a) overlay of all detected couplets aligned to the first PVC peak; (b) couplet count per session; (c) comparison between the median QRS waveform of the first and second PVC within each couplet.\label{fig:couplets}}

\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:couplet_strips}} shows representative couplets in their raw recording
context, using $\pm$5\,s windows and the same visual convention adopted in the previous example
strips. The ECG trace is shown in green, and the two ectopic QRS complexes forming the couplet are
highlighted in red. One clean representative example is shown for each distinct local rhythm motif,
so that the panel illustrates the range of contexts in which couplets occurred.

\reportfig{14_couplet_example_strips.png}{Representative couplets shown in raw recording context over $\pm$5\,s windows, with one example for each distinct local rhythm motif.\label{fig:couplet_strips}}

Local rhythm structure around each couplet was examined by coding the four beats before and after
the couplet as N, for sinus beats, or V, for PVCs. This analysis tests whether couplets occur
randomly within the rhythm or tend to recur within a stereotyped local sequence
(\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:couplet_motifs}}). The most common
motif was \texttt{N V N N [V V] N V N N}, observed in 30 of 46 couplets, corresponding to 65\% of
cases. This pattern represents an organised trigeminy-like cadence, with an isolated PVC, two sinus
beats, the couplet, and then recurrence of the same sequence.

These 30 occurrences were distributed across six recording sessions, with no single session
contributing more than one third of the cases. This suggests that the motif is not driven by a
single recording but reflects a recurring rhythm pattern in the dataset. The pattern was not
universal: the remaining 16 couplets occurred in variant contexts, including after longer sinus
runs, after bigeminal stretches, or before quieter segments. For this reason, the representative
strips include several motifs rather than only the dominant one.

\reportfig{15_couplet_local_rhythm_motifs.png}{Local rhythm motifs surrounding detected couplets, coded as N for sinus beats and V for PVCs. The dominant motif was \texttt{N V N N [V V] N V N N}, present in 30 of 46 couplets.\label{fig:couplet_motifs}}

\section{Cross-session rhythm and burden dynamics}
Cross-session rhythm metrics were recomputed from the curated recordings using the same
definitions applied throughout the report. PVC burden was defined as the number of PVCs divided
by the total number of detected beats. The effective sinus-node rate was estimated as
\texttt{60000 / median N-N interval}, corresponding to the rate expected from the underlying
sinus rhythm rather than the total number of detected QRS complexes.

\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:cross}} summarises four cross-session measures. The first panel shows PVC
burden by session, capturing how the detected ectopic load varies across recordings. The second
panel relates effective sinus-node rate to pause type, comparing the relative proportion of
interpolated and compensated PVCs. The third panel shows the per-session composition of
interpolated and compensated PVCs. The fourth panel summarises coupling stability across
sessions; a tight and session-stable pre-PVC coupling interval supports the interpretation of a
recurrent monomorphic PVC pattern within the limits of this single-lead setup.

\reportfig{09_cross_session_rhythm_and_burden.png}{Cross-session rhythm and burden dynamics: PVC burden by session, effective sinus-node rate versus pause type, per-session pause-type composition, and coupling-interval stability.\label{fig:cross}}

The main cross-session relationship appears in the effective-rate-versus-pause-type panel. The
proportion of compensated PVCs increases with the effective sinus-node rate, whereas the
proportion of interpolated PVCs decreases. Across sessions, this association is strong and
positive for compensated PVCs (Spearman $r\approx0.78$, $p\approx0.008$). In practical terms,
the number of perceptible post-extrasystolic pauses appears to track the underlying sinus rate
more closely than the raw PVC count. The per-session pause distributions underlying this
composition shift are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:psp}.

% ===================== 11. RESPIRATION =====================
\section{Respiration, ECG-derived respiration, and respiratory-phase association}\label{sec:edr}
The final analysis asks whether PVC timing is coupled to respiration. No external respiration belt
was used; instead, respiration was estimated from the ECG itself. Chest movement and respiratory
changes in thoracic impedance modulate the amplitude of the R wave, allowing an ECG-derived
respiration signal to be reconstructed from normal beats. For each session, the R-wave amplitude of
sinus beats was cubic-resampled to 4\,Hz, band-pass filtered between 0.1 and 0.5\,Hz, and converted
into instantaneous respiratory phase using the Hilbert transform. This made it possible to assign
each beat, including each PVC, to a position within the respiratory cycle
(\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:edrdemo}}).

An ECG-derived respiration signal was recovered in all \nSessions{} sessions. PVC timing was
significantly non-uniform across respiratory-phase bins in all sessions, based on a $\chi^2$ test
across phase bins ($p<0.05$). This indicates that PVCs were not randomly distributed across the
respiratory cycle, but were preferentially concentrated at specific respiratory phases. The R-wave
amplitude was largest at full-lung inflation, as confirmed directly during recording, so phase
0/100\% was assigned to end-inspiration and phase 50\% to end-expiration.

Across sessions, PVCs were over-represented near full-lung inflation and sparsest near the
empty-lung phase. The mean peak enrichment was approximately 2.6-fold above chance. This pattern is
consistent with a respiratory mechanical contribution related to lung volume, venous return,
cardiac filling, and filling-related stretch. It does not prove a specific causal mechanism, but it
argues against the earlier assumption that the dominant association occurred at end-expiration.

\reportfig{16_edr_respiration_demo.png}{ECG-derived respiration from sinus-beat R-wave amplitude. The R-wave amplitude traces the respiratory cycle, and PVCs recur preferentially at a specific respiratory phase.\label{fig:edrdemo}}

\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:edrphase}} summarises the phase distribution of all PVCs relative to normal
beats and the per-session enrichment around the respiratory cycle. Values above 1 indicate phases
in which PVCs are over-represented relative to chance. Phase 0/100\% corresponds to full-lung
inflation, or end-inspiration, and phase 50\% corresponds to empty lungs, or end-expiration. The
enrichment peak occurs near full-lung inflation.

\reportfig{17_respiratory_phase_vs_pvc_enrichment.png}{(a) phase distribution of all PVCs versus normal beats; (b) per-session PVC enrichment around the respiratory cycle. Values above 1 mark phases where PVCs are over-represented.\label{fig:edrphase}}

\subsection{Respiratory phase by PVC type}
The respiratory-phase distribution was also examined separately for interpolated PVCs, compensated
PVCs, and couplet PVCs, including both beats of each couplet. Each distribution was normalised
independently, so that phase preference could be compared despite different event counts across
classes.

The phase peaks were 79\% of the cycle for interpolated PVCs, 12\% for compensated PVCs, and 87\%
for couplet PVCs, with phase 0 corresponding to full-lung inflation. Because respiratory phase is
circular, these peaks at 79\%, 12\%, and 87\% all fall within the same sector around the 0/100\%
full-lung boundary, rather than indicating genuinely different respiratory phases. This is consistent
with interpolated, compensated, and coupled PVCs sharing a common respiratory-phase association,
rather than occupying clearly separate respiratory phases (\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:edrtype}}).

\reportfig[0.6\linewidth]{18_respiratory_phase_by_pvc_type.png}{Respiratory-phase distribution by PVC type. Interpolated, compensated and couplet PVCs all show enrichment near the full-lung phase.\label{fig:edrtype}}

respiration quality score. A significant $p$ value indicates that PVCs are not uniformly
distributed across the respiratory cycle. This is a signal-level observation only and should not be
interpreted as a clinical finding.

\section{Robustness of the single-population result}\label{app:clust}
The main text concludes that the detected PVCs form a single continuous monomorphic population rather than discrete morphological subtypes. Because $k$-means returns $k$ clusters whether or not real structure is present, that conclusion was stress-tested with additional checks on the same amplitude-normalised morphology matrix (all \totPVC{} PVCs); these were computed by \texttt{host/analyze\_pvc\_clustering.py}.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Number of clusters.} Silhouette scores were uniformly low and did not improve when more clusters were forced: $0.22$ at $k=2$, $0.20$ at $k=3$, and $0.21$ at $k=4$. Values this close to zero mean that beats sit about as near to neighbouring clusters as to their own, that is, no genuine separation. Forcing $k=3$ merely split the cloud into partitions of roughly 9,300, 5,300 and 2,600 beats with no gap between them, exactly as expected when a single continuous distribution is sliced more finely. A Gaussian-mixture fit gave a monotonically decreasing BIC with $k$ --- the signature of a non-Gaussian \emph{continuous} density rather than discrete clusters --- and its two-component solution was heavily overlapping, with a mean separation of only $0.70$ of the within-component spread and no valley between the components.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{The sparse skirt.} Density-based clustering (DBSCAN) returned one dominant dense cluster ($\sim$86\% of beats) surrounded by a diffuse low-density halo, rather than two comparable dense groups. The thin low-density scatter noted in the main text (\textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:continuum}a}) was profiled directly by isolating the 3\% lowest-density beats: their QRS morphology was indistinguishable from the core (median width 100 vs 100\,ms, amplitude 1.07 vs 1.10\,V, coupling 474 vs 480\,ms), and they were strongly concentrated in the two shortest recordings ($\approx$18--20\% of those sessions' beats, versus $\approx$2\% in the longer ones). This is the signature of recording-quality variability in short sessions, not a second ectopic focus.

\medskip\noindent Together these checks reinforce the single-population interpretation: there is no evidence for a discrete second PVC morphology at any clustering granularity, and the sparse beats reflect signal quality rather than a distinct source.
